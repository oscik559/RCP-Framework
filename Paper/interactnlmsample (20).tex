\begin{abstract}
LLM-based retrieval systems can execute queries and produce fluent answers, yet they rarely preserve a traceable record of what evidence was retrieved, how it was validated, or why synthesis succeeded.
The resulting lack of provenance and auditability limits debugging, compliance verification, and operational trust—critical gaps for engineering applications where correctness depends on interpreting structured specifications within revision-controlled technical documentation. 
We introduce the Relational Control Plane (RCP), a SQL-backed architecture that persists orchestration state as queryable relational records. RCP enforces a six-stage verify-then-summarize control loop in which synthesis is permitted only after retrieved evidence satisfies validation constraints, transforming potential hallucinations into explicit, auditable failures. Domain behaviour is defined through reusable Strategy and Function libraries, enabling configuration via declarative templates rather than modifications to orchestration logic.
% Evaluation across two industrial domains shows statistically significant improvements: answer correctness increases from 44.6\% to 80.0\% while hallucination decreases from 55.4\% to 7.7\%, even with a lightweight language model. These results demonstrate that reliable agentic AI systems emerge from architectural persistence, structured validation, and traceable workflows rather than model scale, enabling deterministic replay and audit-ready provenance for high-stakes deployments.
Evaluation across 200 annotated queries in two industrial domains shows statistically significant improvements: answer correctness increases from 45\% to 85\% and hallucination decreases from 55\% to 5\% in the hydraulic domain; from 23\% to 81\% correctness with hallucination reduced from 47\% to 11\% in the aerospace domain---even with a lightweight language model. These results demonstrate that reliable agentic AI systems emerge from architectural persistence, structured validation, and traceable workflows rather than model scale, enabling deterministic replay and audit-ready provenance for high-stakes deployments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:Introduction}
% \textcolor{red}{Introduction}
%==============================================================================
% Engineering documentation problem
Engineering organizations depend on vast, heterogeneous technical documentation—specifications, drawings, manuals, revision histories, change orders, reports, and standards—dispersed across incompatible systems and repositories. To retrieve precise product, component, and process details, engineers routinely cross-reference multiple sources, interpret technical notation, track supersessions, and reconcile multi-document dependencies—tasks that require tacit domain expertise \cite{hertzum2000information}. Knowledge workers spend roughly 20\% of their time searching for and gathering information \cite{court1997supporting, mckinsey2012social}, often resorting to asking colleagues because navigating heterogeneous document repositories remains prohibitively difficult.

% Engineering changes + LLM promise
Engineering changes consume 30–50\% of product development effort \cite{fricke2000coping}, with individual modifications cascading unpredictably across interconnected components—triggering revisions to drawings, bills of materials, tooling specifications, and test procedures \cite{clarkson2004predicting}. Despite decades of knowledge management research \cite{mcmahon2004knowledge, heisig2010exploring}, the gap between what engineers need and what retrieval systems deliver persists. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have enabled new approaches to extracting and reasoning over such information sources.

% RAG limitations
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) grounds LLM outputs in retrieved evidence \cite{lewis2020rag}, yet production deployments reveal systematic failure modes
that are particularly acute for engineering modalities such as unit-bearing
specifications, structured tables, and revision-controlled documentation. Hallucination remains structurally inherent to LLM generation \cite{huang2025hallucinationsurvey, kalai2025hallucinate}, and user studies in manufacturing confirm operators still prefer consulting human experts over autonomous LLM-generated answers \cite{kernanfreire2024knowledge}.

% Agentic frameworks insufficient
Agentic frameworks address some of these limitations through iterative reasoning and tool use \cite{yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting, wang2024autonomousagents}. However, engineering tasks require coordinating multiple specialized agents—such as table extraction, revision tracking, and standards interpretation—each operating over distinct data sources with independent validation requirements. This coordination demands persistent state management across reasoning episodes \cite{packer2023memgpt}, structured validation gates that prevent unsupported synthesis \cite{madaan2023selfrefine}, and fine-grained source attribution linking each claim to specific document locations \cite{rashkin2023attribution}. Existing multi-agent frameworks such as AutoGen \cite{wu2024autogen} and LangGraph provide graph-based orchestration abstractions, but leave persistence, validation, and deterministic replay to application-level implementation.

% Contribution
This paper presents a domain-agnostic agentic framework for multi-agent orchestration in engineering applications. We externalize orchestration state into a SQL-backed relational persistence model, enabling deterministic replay, full observability, and audit-ready traceability. Rather than maintaining transient in-memory execution graphs, all orchestration artifacts—goals, strategies, function calls, parameters, outputs, and validation outcomes—are stored as queryable relational records.

\vspace{1em}
\noindent The contributions of this work are threefold:
\begin{itemize}[label=-]
    \item A persistent Relational Control Plane (RCP) architecture that validates retrieved evidence before synthesis, storing goals, strategies, and validation outcomes as queryable relational records to enable deterministic replay and audit-ready provenance.
    \item A domain-agnostic design based on reusable Strategy and Function libraries, demonstrated across two industrial domains without modifying core orchestration logic.
    % \item An empirical evaluation on 65 engineering queries showing that the RCP framework increases answer correctness from 44.6\% to 80.0\% and reduces hallucination from 55.4\% to 7.7\% compared to RAG and non-persistent agentic baselines.
     \item An empirical evaluation on 200 engineering queries (100 per industrial domain) showing that the RCP framework increases answer correctness from 45\% to 85\% and reduces hallucination from 55\% to 5\% in the hydraulic domain, and from 23\% to 81\% correctness with hallucination reduced from 47\% to 11\% in the aerospace domain, compared to RAG and SQL-backed retrieval baselines.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related_work}
%============================================================================== 
\subsection{Agentic AI in Engineering} 
\label{sec:agentic-ai-engineering} %============================================================================== 
Agentic AI systems extend beyond one-shot generation by iteratively planning, invoking external tools, and executing intermediate actions toward a goal \cite{wang2024autonomousagents, prasad2024adapt}. The ReAct paradigm \cite{yao2023reactsynergizingreasoningacting} established interleaved reasoning and acting as a practical template, while multi-agent frameworks such as AutoGen \cite{wu2024autogen} and LangGraph provide graph-based abstractions for coordinating specialised agents. Such systems are increasingly investigated in manufacturing and engineering contexts, where multi-agent coordination and goal-oriented action are required \cite{leng2026, zhao2025}. In engineering document comprehension, agent-based architectures have been proposed to dynamically route queries to specialised agents, motivating modular designs for engineering knowledge extraction \cite{xie2026agentframework}. Empirical work demonstrates both capabilities and limitations: Weibull et al.~\cite{weibull_named_2025} report near-perfect named entity extraction from industrial diagrams using multimodal LLMs, whereas Li et al.~\cite{li2025a} identify persistent issues including specification oversimplification and fabrication when extracting formal specifications from documentation.
 
Two challenges consistently emerge: hallucination and reasoning degradation under extended context. Wei et al.~\cite{wei2026shadows} attribute hallucination to internal-state drift caused by incremental context injection and ``attention-locking'' after multiple interaction rounds. Xie and Ju~\cite{xie2026agentframework} further highlight the limited domain-specific reasoning ability of general-purpose LLMs and argue that constructing curated models for every engineering subdomain is impractical, motivating structured retrieval mechanisms. Reliability challenges---including response inconsistency and limited interpretability---are further discussed by Leng et al.~\cite{leng2026}, who conclude that agentic systems require explicit validation mechanisms rather than black-box reasoning.

However, multi-agent frameworks such as AutoGen and LangGraph leave persistence, validation, and deterministic replay to application-level implementation. Packer et al.~\cite{packer2023memgpt} demonstrate the value of externalising memory beyond the context window via a virtual context management layer, yet MemGPT does not enforce structured validation gates over retrieved evidence. Madaan et al.~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine} show that iterative self-refinement improves output quality, but their approach operates over free-form language feedback rather than typed, schema-level predicates. Collectively, these findings motivate architectures that enforce structured interaction, persistent state, and verifiable evidence---allowing intermediate reasoning steps to be inspected and validated rather than implicitly assumed.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Document Understanding for Structured Extraction}
\label{sec:document_understanding}
%==============================================================================
Engineering knowledge is predominantly captured in documents containing heterogeneous elements: dense tables, technical drawings, figures, flowcharts, algorithms, multi-column layouts, and revision annotations. Extracting structured information from these sources requires layout-aware models that preserve spatial and semantic relationships \cite{zhang2025doc}. Surface-level text extraction is insufficient because critical information is encoded in table structure/notes, cross-cell references, figure captions, measurement units, and domain-specific notation \cite{guo2026dtbench, foppiano2019}.

Document AI has advanced through transformer-based architectures and specialised recognition models, including Vision Language Models (VLMs). Xu et al.~\cite{xu2020} introduced LayoutLM for joint text--layout modelling; Table Transformer \cite{lin2022tsrformer, nassar2022tableformer} and  DOTABLER \cite{li2025surface} address table structure recognition; and Safder et al.~\cite{safder2022} demonstrated figure parsing methods for semantic enrichment.

Technical documents introduce additional complexities beyond static layout. Revision markers alter content validity non-locally---a superseded value may appear typographically identical to an active one, requiring applicability metadata to be preserved at extraction time rather than inferred at query time \cite{xie2026agentframework}. Mixed units require normalisation, and cross-references between drawings and parts lists demand anchored, location-aware extraction. Modern multimodal document parsers combine OCR, layout analysis, and entity linking, but often lose semantic anchors---such as page numbers, footnote references, table cell coordinates, and figure callouts---needed for provenance tracking. These challenges motivate extraction pipelines that preserve provenance anchors while transforming unstructured documents into queryable relational structures.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Hybrid and Multi-Step Retrieval}
\label{sec:hybrid_multistep_retrieval}
%==============================================================================
RAG \cite{lewis2020rag} is a foundational mechanism for grounding LLM outputs in
external knowledge, yet production deployments reveal systematic failure modes:
chunking strategies fragment dense table structures, multi-document consolidation
fails when evidence is scattered, and retrieval mechanisms optimised for prose
underperform on unit-bearing specifications and structured drawings
\cite{barnett2024seven}. These limitations are amplified in engineering
applications requiring synthesis across modalities, document structures, and
multiple sources \cite{gao2023retrieval, xie2026agentframework}.

One response has been to pre-structure source knowledge before retrieval.
Approaches that objectify unstructured documents into queryable relational or
graph schemas improve retrieval precision by preserving field semantics and
enabling constraint-based lookup \cite{aster2024langchainsql}. GraphRAG
\cite{mondal2025} and MDKG-RAG \cite{zhang2025} use graph traversal over
structured knowledge graphs; TaxFlow \cite{Karna31122026} combines metadata
indexing with validity filtering for legal texts---analogous to engineering
revision control; and HGMem \cite{zhou2026} employs external memory capturing
higher-order correlations to address fragmented reasoning in extended retrieval
contexts. Hao et al.~\cite{hao2021} demonstrate that semantically structured
knowledge representations outperform conventional keyword-based search. These
approaches strengthen retrieval fidelity but are often architecturally rigid,
since filtering, traversal, and synthesis are largely predefined within the
system design, with no persistent record of intermediate validation outcomes.

Parallel work has framed multi-step retrieval as a sequential decision-making problem. Planner--executor architectures decompose complex queries into intermediate sub-goals executed iteratively by specialised reasoning and retrieval components \cite{Liu2025OPERAAR}. Reinforcement-learning-based approaches \cite{Song2025R1SearcherIT, jin2025searchr, zhang2024beam, sorokin2026qrag} train LLMs to generate intermediate search queries, enabling more adaptive retrieval. While effective, these methods are computationally expensive and do not persist intermediate validation outcomes or provide staged verification at multiple steps, making it difficult to inspect why a chain succeeded, where it failed, or how it should be replayed and audited in engineering settings---a governance gap that motivates the present approach.


Several systems combine relational storage with LLM reasoning in ways that partially overlap with the proposed approach. LangChain's SQL retriever and LlamaIndex's SQL query engine support direct database querying from natural language \cite{aster2024langchainsql}, but treat SQL as a retrieval mechanism rather than a persistence layer for orchestration state---execution logic remains in transient memory with no multi-level validation. OPERA-AR \cite{Liu2025OPERAAR} introduces a planner--executor design with intermediate sub-goals, but does not persist validation outcomes or support deterministic replay. Emerging skill-based abstractions such as declarative Agent Skill specifications \cite{agentskills2024} define modular, discoverable capability manifests, but do not address relational validation or execution provenance. The RCP differs from these systems in two respects: (i)~orchestration artefacts---goal definitions, strategy selections, function invocations, and validation outcomes---are first-class persistent records rather than ephemeral execution traces, and (ii)~progression through the reasoning loop is gated by explicit validation predicates at the function, strategy, and goal levels, rather than being left to a single final generation step. This distinction is what enables post-hoc inspection, replay, and governance in deployment contexts.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Evaluating Multi-Step Reasoning}
\label{sec:evaluating_multistep_reasoning}
%==============================================================================
Traditional question--answering benchmarks such as OK-VQA, WebQA, and HotpotQA emphasise final answer accuracy, masking failure modes that are critical for engineering auditability---such as retrieval errors and step omission \cite{marino2019okvqa, chang2021webqa, yang2018hotpotqa}. While useful for model comparison, these benchmarks lack the fine-grained, step-level annotations needed to attribute errors to specific retrieval or reasoning stages.

More recent benchmarks address these limitations. Ning et al.~\cite{ning2026mcsearch} introduce MC-SEARCH, a framework for agentic multimodal RAG with step-wise annotated chains spanning five reasoning topologies, proposing HAVE verification with process-level metrics. RAGEval \cite{zhu2025rageval} builds on this direction by introducing Completeness, Hallucination, and Irrelevance metrics for scenario-specific RAG evaluation beyond conventional accuracy measures. However, process metrics alone are insufficient: observability requires persistent execution traces for post-hoc analysis \cite{moshkovich2025agentic}, and controlled field studies show that trust in AI outputs remains a barrier to adoption even when accuracy is high \cite{lowhagen2025aiassistant}. Crucially, none of these benchmarks targets the correctness criteria central to domain-specific agentic retrieval---unit fidelity, revision applicability, and citation granularity---beyond semantic answer matching \cite{jafari2025measurement}. This motivates the evaluation design in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:Method}
%=====================================
The proposed framework addresses the requirements identified in Section~\ref{sec:related_work} through a six-stage control loop backed by a relational persistence layer. The following subsections detail the persistent data structures and libraries that support this architecture.
%_________________________________________________________________________________________

\subsection{Storing Engineering Data}
\label{subsec:storing_engineering_data}
To accommodate the heterogeneity of engineering documentation, the framework extracts and structures source material into typed data instances paired with explicit provenance anchors (page, section, paragraph, figure/captions, and table-cell/notes references). The resulting storage model has two tiers: a persistent knowledge base that normalises core engineering data into relational form, and a transient, session-scoped cache for run-time assembly. As shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:harvesting_pipeline}, raw documents are decomposed into structured content units (sections, tables, figures, and metadata) before being stored in the Harvested database.
 
% Harvested / Structured Database (Objectification Layer)
The \emph{Harvested} database serves as the canonical repository of engineering knowledge extracted from source documents. Across the domains tested in this work, raw materials---PDFs, scanned documents, and manuals---are converted into a normalised relational form through a process referred to here as \emph{objectification}: the systematic transformation of unstructured document content into typed relational entities with preserved provenance. Objectification ensures that:
(a)~semantic structure and entities follow a consistent relational schema across documents,
(b)~numerical and unit-bearing fields are stored with canonical types,
(c)~provenance (page, section, figure, or table anchors) is retained, and
(d)~all records are indexed to support deterministic, key-based retrieval.
The Harvested database is stable across sessions and free of reasoning artefacts, supporting incremental extension and reproducible querying.

% Temporary Database (Session Assembly Layer)
During reasoning, strategy plans often require intermediate aggregation, filtering, or transformation of data that should not modify the persistent knowledge base. For this purpose, the framework constructs a temporary, session-scoped database at the start of each reasoning episode (described in Section~\ref{subsec:relational_control_plane}). This store holds:
(a)~intermediate subsets produced by search or filtering agents,
(b)~normalised or reformatted values required for downstream validation, and
(c)~transient multi-step artefacts such as assembled evidence sets.
 
The temporary database is strictly short-lived: its contents exist only for the duration of the reasoning episode and are destroyed at session end. Before destruction, all intermediate artefacts are copied as persistent records into the RCP, ensuring that the complete execution trail is recoverable from the RCP alone.
 
%==============================================================================
\subsection{Relational Control Plane (RCP)}
\label{subsec:relational_control_plane}
%==============================================================================
The RCP serves as the central orchestration database of the proposed framework, externalising coordination logic from code into a normalised relational SQL schema (SQLite for prototyping, PostgreSQL for production). It stores orchestration artefacts---goals, strategies, function invocations, parameters, outputs, and validation outcomes---as first-class relational entities rather than unstructured logs or ephemeral runtime state.
 
The RCP enforces a separation between design-time policy and run-time execution. At design time, \emph{library tables} define the system's capability space: strategies are represented as reusable plan templates, while functions are modelled as atomic operations with declared input--output contracts. Each function's contract is formalised through two companion library tables: \texttt{FunctionParametersLibrary}, which declares the expected input parameters (name, type, and constraints), and \texttt{FunctionOutputLibrary}, which declares the expected output schema (name, type, and postconditions). Together with the \texttt{FunctionLibrary} entry itself, these three tables constitute the complete typed contract for each atomic operation. At run time, corresponding \emph{instance tables} record each concrete goal, strategy, and function invocation, including their parameters, outputs, and validation outcomes. This separation allows behaviour to be authored once and reused across domains while making every orchestration decision a persistent, queryable record.

                \State $\sigma_{\text{out}} \gets L_{FO}.\text{GetSchema}(f)$ \Comment{Declared output contract}
                \State \text{Validate}($Step.\text{Inputs},\;\sigma_{\text{in}}$) \Comment{Pre-execution check}
                \State $O \gets f.\text{Execute}(Step.\text{Inputs})$
                \State $\text{RCP.\,LogExecution}(f, O)$
                \State \textbf{Stage 4: Function validation} \label{alg:stage4}
                \If{$\neg \text{ValidateFunction}(O,\;\sigma_{\text{out}})$} \Comment{Post-execution check}
                    \State \textbf{retry} $Step$ with adjusted params.\ or \textbf{break}
                \EndIf
                \State \textbf{Stage 5: Strategy validation} \label{alg:stage5}
                \If{$\text{ValidateStrategy}(S, O)$}
                    \State \textbf{break} \Comment{Sufficiency criteria met early}
                \EndIf
            \EndFor
            \State
            \State \textbf{Stage 6: Goal validation} \label{alg:stage6}
            \If{$\text{ValidateGoal}(G, S.\text{FinalOutput})$}
                \State \Return $A \gets S.\text{FinalOutput}$
            \Else
                \State \textbf{backtrack} to Stage~2 or \Return $E$
            \EndIf
            \end{algorithmic}
        \end{algorithm}
    \end{minipage}

Upon receiving a query, the controller instantiates a goal record with explicit success criteria---such as revision scope, evidence granularity, or citation requirements---and selects an appropriate strategy instance according to stored policies in the RCP libraries. The controller then dispatches one or more agents whose input--output contracts match the current system state. Typed contracts are enforced within the RCP: each function template defines its expected input and output schemas via \texttt{FunctionParametersLibrary} and \texttt{FunctionOutputLibrary} and preconditions, while validation outcomes at the function, strategy, and goal levels are persisted as structured records in the instance tables.

Progress through the loop is validation-centric: advancement is permitted only when defined predicates---such as unit consistency, revision applicability, or citation completeness---are satisfied. All computational artefacts (structured tables, retrieved passages, intermediate results, and evidence links) are stored within the RCP, while the orchestration graph maintains only lightweight references (active goal, strategy, and function identifiers). This arrangement minimises in-memory state and enables reproducible runs through standard relational queries. Note that while the artefact trail is fully reproducible, decision steps involving LLM inference (strategy selection, synthesis) may vary across runs due to the inherent non-determinism of language model generation.

Compared to conventional agentic frameworks in which orchestration logic and execution order are typically embedded in application code, the RCP design offers three structural properties:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textit{Data-driven policy.} Changes in execution order, eligibility, or capability preference (for example, inserting a lineage-checking step before finalisation or prioritising hierarchical retrieval over keyword search) are achieved through updates to library records rather than code rewrites.
    \item \textit{Full artefact persistence.} Every decision, artefact, and validation outcome is recorded, supporting post-hoc analysis and quantitative evaluation of strategy performance.
    \item \textit{Modular extensibility.} New capabilities or alternative implementations (e.g., retrievers, table parsers, or unit normalisation tools) can be registered in the RCP library; existing strategies incorporate them without altering the orchestration graph.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Strategy Library}
\label{subsec:strategy_library}
%==============================================================================
The Strategy Library contains reusable plan templates that standardise patterns of tool usage, reasoning, and validation across engineering contexts. Each strategy specifies four core components:
\begin{itemize}[label=- ]
    \item \text{Targets:} The evidence or variables required for successful completion (e.g., numerical value with units, revision identifier, document citation).
    \item \text{Eligible functions:} The permissible computational modules and their preferred execution order (e.g., search $\rightarrow$ parse $\rightarrow$ normalise $\rightarrow$ verify).
    \item \text{Sufficiency conditions:} Formal predicates that must hold before a strategy is considered complete (e.g., at least two corroborating sources; explicit revision supersession confirmed).
    \item \text{Fallback policies:} Adaptive behaviours when sufficiency is not met (e.g., expand search scope, trigger hierarchical retrieval).
\end{itemize}
For example, a \emph{Specification Lookup} strategy declares: target = product attribute with unit; plan = [extract product code $\rightarrow$ retrieve record $\rightarrow$ extract attribute $\rightarrow$ validate]; sufficiency = attribute resolved and unit normalised. Concrete instantiations for each case study are presented in Sections~\ref{subsec:hydroscand_case} and~\ref{subsec:saab_case}.

%==============================================================================
\subsection{Function Library}
\label{subsec:function_library}
%==============================================================================
The Function Library contains modular, atomic operations that execute the steps defined in strategy plans. Each function is a self-contained component with declared inputs, outputs, and postconditions. Functions do not modify the persistent Harvested database; their outputs are written exclusively to session-scoped RCP instance tables, ensuring that the knowledge base remains stable across sessions.
 
Each function's interface is formalised through two companion library tables. \texttt{FunctionParametersLibrary} declares the expected input schema---parameter names, types, and constraints (e.g., \texttt{product\_code: string, required}). \texttt{FunctionOutputLibrary} declares the expected output schema---output names, types, and postconditions (e.g., \texttt{extracted\_data: json, non-empty}). At execution time, the controller validates actual inputs against \texttt{FunctionParametersLibrary} before invocation and validates actual outputs against \texttt{FunctionOutputLibrary} afterwards, enabling early detection of schema violations before downstream functions are invoked.
 
Typical functions include: text retrieval (keyword/semantic search with constraint support), hierarchical retrieval (document tree traversal), table extraction (region detection with cell anchoring), unit normalisation (canonical conversion with provenance), revision checking (supersession tracing), figure linking, keyword expansion, table filtering, and evidence scoring.
 
Each function produces validated artefacts (values, units, revisions, citations) that feed into a strategy's target set. Validation gates determine whether these artefacts satisfy sufficiency conditions; when unmet, the orchestration layer invokes additional or alternative functions. Because all functions adhere to uniform interface specifications, components can be replaced or added by registering them in the Function Library---no changes to the orchestration graph are required.



%==============================================================================
\subsection{In-Session Instantiation}
\label{subsec:in-session_instantiation}
%==============================================================================
At runtime, design-time templates are instantiated as session-scoped records that form the execution ledger. For every user query, the controller creates a \texttt{GoalInSession} record with explicit acceptance criteria. It then selects and materialises a \texttt{StrategyInSession} record linked to the goal via foreign key. Within this strategy context, the controller instantiates one or more functions; each invocation generates three linked record sets:
 
\begin{itemize}[label=-]
    \item \texttt{FunctionInSession}: the invocation record (function identity, strategy link, success status).
    \item \texttt{FunctionParametersInSession}: one row per input parameter, validated against the declared schema in \texttt{FunctionParametersLibrary}.
    \item \texttt{FunctionOutputInSession}: one row per output value, validated against the declared schema in \texttt{FunctionOutputLibrary}.
\end{itemize}
 
\noindent Foreign-key constraints enforce a strict hierarchy: every function instance references a strategy instance, which in turn references a goal instance. This means that any record---an output value, a validation outcome, a parameter binding---can be traced back to the originating goal through at most two joins. The orchestration graph itself maintains only lightweight references (active goal, strategy, and function identifiers); all heavy artefacts reside in the RCP instance tables.

\subsubsection{Strategy Selection Mechanism}
\label{subsubsec:strategy_selection_mechanism}
The controller selects strategies through an LLM-guided evaluation. Upon instantiating a goal, the controller presents the LLM with the current query, a structured goal description, and the ordered list of available strategy templates (excluding those already attempted in the current session). The LLM is prompted to select the most appropriate strategy based on the following criteria:
\begin{itemize}[label=-]
    \item \text{Entity recognition:} Does the query contain a component or product identifier?
    \item \text{Attribute type:} Is the target a simple lookup (single value), comparison (multiple entities), or aggregation (product family)?
    \item \text{Evidence requirements:} Does the goal require revision awareness, cross-document reasoning, or multimodal synthesis?
    \item \text{Data availability:} Are authoritative documents, sections, or paragraph references identified, or is exploratory search required?
\end{itemize}
 
\noindent The LLM returns a single strategy name drawn from the available list; the selection and its accompanying rationale are persisted to the RCP as a structured record, providing an auditable account of each selection decision. If no valid strategy is returned after retries, the controller falls back to a broad retrieval strategy that combines keyword and semantic search across all available documents, or returns a failure diagnostic. Because selection criteria are embedded in the prompt rather than in hard-coded predicates, new strategies are registered by adding a named template to the Strategy Library---no changes to the controller are required.
 
The LLM-guided selection step is inherently non-deterministic: given identical inputs, the selected strategy may vary across runs. This is a known trade-off of the current design. While it provides flexibility and avoids hard-coded routing logic, it means that strategy selection is the one component of the framework that is not fully data-driven. A planned enhancement replaces this step with a rule-based precondition mechanism in which each strategy template declares explicit eligibility predicates, making selection fully deterministic and independently auditable.

\subsubsection{Computational Characteristics}
\label{subsubsec:computational_characteristics}
Under the planned rule-based selection, strategy matching is $O(S \cdot P)$, where $S$ is the number of strategy templates and $P$ is the average number of precondition checks per template; in practice $S < 20$ for domain-specific instantiations, making selection overhead negligible. In the current LLM-guided implementation, selection cost is dominated by a single LLM inference call (typically 2K--4K context tokens).
 
Function execution cost is dominated by external operations: SQL retrieval runs in $O(\log N)$ for indexed lookups to $O(N)$ for unindexed full-text scans over $N$ documents, and LLM inference scales linearly with context length (typically 2K--8K tokens per call with KV-cache). For a session with $G$ goals, $S$ strategy instances, and $F$ function calls, RCP storage is $O(G + S + F \cdot (I + O))$, where $I$ and $O$ are average input and output sizes per function. Measured across both case studies, typical sessions generate 5--20 function calls with aggregate storage under 1~MB per query.

\subsection{Case I: Industrial Hydraulic Products Catalogue (Hydroscand AB)}
\label{subsec:hydroscand_case}
%===============================================================
Industrial hydraulic systems rely on extensive product catalogues that consolidate specifications for hoses, fittings, and coupling assemblies. These documents encode critical attributes---dimensions, pressure ratings, reinforcement structures, material compositions, and thread standards. The \emph{Hydraulic Produktbok}~\cite{hydroscand2020produktbok}, a widely used Nordic fluid-power reference, is representative: it organises chapter-level product families, construction descriptions, and dense specification tables spanning numerous variants within each product line.

The catalogue's structure poses notable challenges for automated extraction. Variant-level data appear in compact tables with merged cells, multi-row headers, mixed unit conventions, and visually implied hierarchies. Thread standards (BSP/G, JIC, ORFS, NPTF) depend on subtle typographic distinctions that OCR and na\"ive PDF parsers often distort, and free-text descriptions---entirely in Swedish---use specialised terminology that impedes generic retrieval models.

As a result, chunk-based RAG tends to lose table semantics, misinterpret values, or fail to deliver cell-level, engineering-grade citations. Queries based on technical specifications (pressure-rating lookup, thread-compatibility checks, application-based product selection) require domain-grounded retrieval that preserves field structure and provenance.

\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}
%============================================
\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:experimental_setup}
%============================================
We evaluate the proposed RCP-based agentic framework on two industrial case studies using an annotated query set of 100 technical questions per case study (200 queries total). To isolate the contribution of each architectural layer, we compare three systems in a layered evaluation design:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\texttt{B\arabic*:}, leftmargin=3.5em]
    \item \textit{Naive RAG.} Standard retrieval-augmented generation: chunk source text (800~characters, 100-character overlap), embed with \textit{qwen3-embedding:8b}\footnote{Alibaba Cloud Qwen3; publicly available via Ollama: \url{https://ollama.com/library/qwen3-embedding}}, store in an in-memory ChromaDB vectorstore, retrieve top-5 chunks, and generate with a single LLM call. No structured schema, no multi-step reasoning.
    \item \textit{SQL-backed Retrieval.} Single-step retrieval over the structured \texttt{harvested.db} schema (categories $\rightarrow$ families $\rightarrow$ products). Product codes are extracted from the query via pattern matching, and direct SQL joins retrieve complete specification records with provenance (page, family, category). Results are passed to the same LLM in a single generation step. This baseline isolates the value of \emph{objectification}---structured relational storage---without multi-step reasoning or validation.
    \item \textit{RCP Framework (Proposed).} The full six-stage control loop with persistent relational state, strategy selection, iterative function execution, and multi-level validation gates.
\end{enumerate}

All methods operate over the same harvested document corpus (\texttt{harvested.db}) and use \emph{Llama~3.2 (3B)} (Ollama~v0.17.7, temperature~$= 0$) for all generation tasks. The Qwen3 embedding model was selected for B1 as a competitive open-weight encoder available through the same local inference stack. The compact LLM was chosen to reflect resource-constrained industrial deployment conditions. Because the RCP's hallucination reduction stems from architectural validation gates that operate over structured artefacts independently of the LLM, we hypothesise that gains would be preserved or amplified with stronger models; confirming this remains future work.

For each case study, we report corpus scale (pages/tables), evaluation scale (number of queries), and per-query compute footprint (LLM calls, tokens, runtime). Implementation code is available via the Data Availability Statement.

\subsection{Quantitative Metrics}
\label{subsec:quantitative_metrics}
%============================================
We report five metrics across both case studies:
(a)~\textit{answer correctness} against the annotated reference set;
(b)~\textit{citation accuracy}---whether the cited passage or table cell contains the claimed value, including units where applicable;
(c)~\textit{unit fidelity}, including correct normalisation and conversions;
(d)~\textit{hallucination rate}---the fraction of responses containing at least one factual claim unsupported by retrieved evidence; and
(e)~\textit{operational cost}, measured in end-to-end latency and token usage.

SQL-backed retrieval (B2) achieves the lowest latency---0.48\,s in Case~I and 2.55\,s in Case~II---because it executes a single deterministic SQL join. The Naive RAG baseline (B1) is slower in Case~I (12.24\,s) than B3's Case~II counterpart, reflecting the cost of embedding all catalogue chunks at query time; in Case~II, B1 requires only document-level chunk embedding over a smaller corpus. The latency--reliability trade-off is inherent to the RCP design: validation gates and persistent state tracking are the mechanisms that eliminate hallucination, and each requires additional computation. In domains where correctness is a compliance or safety requirement, the additional latency of B3 is the cost of auditability.

%============================================
\subsection{Error Analysis}
\label{subsec:error_analysis}
%============================================
Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:qs_hydroscand} and~\ref{tab:qs_saab} list sample queries from the annotated sets used in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}. The test queries, representative of practical use scenarios, were provided by engineers at the respective companies with no involvement in framework development, preventing evaluation leakage.

Each query is assigned a difficulty tier reflecting the number of reasoning steps and source documents required for a correct answer (\emph{Easy}: single table lookup; \emph{Medium}: cross-field inference or semantic matching; \emph{Hard}: multi-document reasoning or domain-specific disambiguation). Tier distributions are reported in the appendix tables; a stratified performance breakdown by tier is reserved for future work.

The \emph{Ground truth} column records the correct answer as determined by the domain expert with reference to the source documents. For Case~I, ground-truth answers were verified against the Hydroscand Produktbok PDF (hydraulic hose and press coupling chapters), whereas ground-truth answers for Case~II were verified against the applicable revision of the Saab technical source document.

The \emph{Error class} column identifies the primary failure mode of the baselines on each query, drawn from the following taxonomy:
\begin{enumerate}[label=\roman*.]
    \item Hallucination---the model asserts a specific value (article number, torque, revision) not present in any retrieved chunk.
    \item Partial answer---a correct product family or category is identified but the required specificity (dimension, standard designation, per-revision caveat) is absent.
    \item Field-name mismatch---the correct source table is retrieved but the query term does not match the column header literally, causing the retrieval step to return no evidence.
    \item Parser error---the source document structure could not be reliably parsed, resulting in malformed or missing field values.
\end{enumerate}

The B1$\rightarrow$B2 transition reveals that the dominant failure mode in Naive RAG is retrieval fragmentation: chunks rarely contain complete specification records, causing the LLM to hallucinate values for missing fields (hallucination rate 55\% in Case~I, 47\% in Case~II). Structured SQL retrieval reduces hallucination to 26\% in Case~I and 12\% in Case~II by delivering complete product records with preserved field semantics, lifting answer correctness from 45\% to 73\% (Case~I) and from 23\% to 76\% (Case~II). Residual B2 errors fall into two classes: keyword-search failures on semantic queries without explicit product codes (where LIKE-pattern fallback retrieves irrelevant records), and generation errors when multiple products are returned and the LLM conflates their attributes.

The B2$\rightarrow$B3 transition shows further improvement. Answer correctness rises to 85\% (Case~I) and 81\% (Case~II) while hallucination is nearly eliminated (5\% in Case~I, 11\% in Case~II). The RCP's multi-step planning enables it to handle both direct lookups and comparison queries through specialised strategies (\emph{Specification Lookup}, \emph{Multi-Product Comparison}), while validation gates prevent the LLM from fabricating plausible but unsupported values. The residual hallucination rate occurs solely in the LLM synthesis step, where validated attributes are converted to natural language. This verify-then-summarise behaviour is the intended design outcome: in engineering contexts, an explicit qualified answer is preferable to a confident but incorrect output.

%============================================
\subsubsection{Citation Accuracy Gap}
\label{subsubsection:citation_accuracy_gap}
%============================================
The RCP's validation gates enforce schema completeness, attribute presence, and unit correctness, but the current predicates do not include an explicit \emph{citation-completeness} check. Consequently, the RCP may produce correct answers grounded in verified attributes without citing the specific catalogue page or table cell. The Naive RAG baseline, by contrast, inherently propagates source references because retrieved chunks carry metadata (document name, page, offset) that the LLM can echo. Adding a citation-provenance predicate to strategy-level sufficiency conditions is a planned extension.

%============================================
\subsubsection{Unit Fidelity Gap}
\label{subsubsection:unit_fidelity_gap}
%============================================
The unit fidelity drop from B2 (93\%) to B3 (88\%) in Case~I follows the same verify-then-summarise logic. B2 retrieves a complete product record via SQL and passes it directly to the LLM, which echoes whatever unit representation is present in the source data---including cases where the source already uses a canonical format. B3, by contrast, routes all values through \texttt{func\_normalize\_units} and enforces a unit-validation predicate before synthesis. When normalisation produces an ambiguous or unresolvable result (e.g., a non-standard thread notation or a missing unit field), the validation gate returns a qualified non-answer rather than propagating the raw value. This reduces hallucination at the cost of a lower raw fidelity score: some unit values that B2 passes through unchecked would have been accepted as correct under the evaluation rubric, whereas B3 explicitly rejects them. Unit fidelity is perfect across all baselines in Case~II, reflecting the more standardised unit notation in aerospace connector documentation. Adding tolerance bands or graduated unit-check predicates is a planned extension.
%============================================


% DISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
%============================================
\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:Discussion}
%============================================
The evaluation demonstrates a clear trade-off: the RCP improves reliability and traceability of LLM-based retrieval in engineering settings, but at measurable computational cost. Average latency rises from 12.24\,s (B1) to 13.65\,s (B3) in Case~I, and from 5.25\,s to 12.84\,s in Case~II, reflecting additional function calls, validation checkpoints, and persistence of intermediate artefacts.

In both case studies, the baselines occasionally produced confident but incorrect outputs, particularly in revision-controlled scenarios. The RCP's verify-then-summarise behaviour is the mechanism behind this improvement: explicit failure when sufficiency conditions are unmet is preferable to silent misinformation in contexts where unit or factual errors propagate downstream. This confirms the motivation advanced by Leng et al.~\cite{leng2026}, who argue that agentic systems require explicit validation mechanisms rather than black-box reasoning, and extends the self-refinement approach of Madaan et al.~\cite{madaan2023selfrefine} from free-form language feedback to typed, schema-level predicates over structured artefacts.

In domains where outputs inform compliance or design decisions, correctness and auditability are primary requirements, and the observed latency overhead (B3 adds 1--4\,s over B1 in both cases) may be acceptable. A practical consequence of the RCP's relational design is that inspection, error attribution, and replay reduce to database queries rather than bespoke logging, lowering the engineering effort associated with debugging, evaluation, and governance in production deployments.

The current evaluation uses a single compact model (Llama~3.2 (3B)). Because validation predicates operate over structured artefacts independently of the LLM, the architectural benefits are expected to hold across model families; however, the absolute correctness and hallucination rates will vary with model capability, and cross-model evaluation remains future work.

%============================================
\vspace{1.0em}
\noindent \textbf{Limitations.}
%============================================

 \vspace{1.0em}
    \noindent \textit{Upstream extraction quality:} System reliability depends on document parsing. OCR errors, poor table reconstruction, and ambiguous typography propagate into the harvested database and constrain downstream reasoning. The RCP prevents unsupported synthesis but cannot correct malformed source data. Advances in multimodal document parsing are likely to improve both extraction and retrieval fidelity.
\vspace{0.5em}

    \noindent \textit{Predicate tuning:} Validation predicates require careful calibration. Overly strict predicates increase retries and latency; overly permissive predicates risk allowing subtle applicability or revision errors to pass. Predicate design thus represents an engineering trade-off between computational cost and error tolerance.
\vspace{0.5em}

    \noindent \textit{Domain scope:} The evaluation was limited to two industrial domains. The domain-agnostic claim pertains to the core orchestration architecture; strategy and function libraries require domain-specific authoring and do not transfer automatically across document genres. Broader benchmarking---across legal contracts, medical device documentation, or legacy scanned manuals---would strengthen the generalisability claim.
\vspace{0.5em}

    \noindent \textit{Extensibility overhead:} The current implementation models agent capabilities as canonical functions registered in a structured function library. While this supports deterministic execution, it introduces integration overhead when extending to new domains. Emerging skill-based capability abstractions~\cite{agentskills2024} that define modular, discoverable capability manifests could reduce this friction while preserving governance guarantees.
\vspace{0.5em}

    \noindent \textit{Cross-session learning:} The framework does not adapt strategy selection or function parameters based on historical execution outcomes; each session starts from a clean state. Incorporating cross-session learning---for example, biasing strategy selection toward templates that have historically succeeded for structurally similar queries---is planned future work.


% CONCLUSION
%============================================
\section{Conclusions and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusions_and_future_work}
%============================================
This study introduced the Relational Control Plane (RCP), a SQL-backed agentic architecture that persists orchestration state---goals, strategies, function invocations, and validation outcomes---as queryable relational records, enforcing a six-stage verify-then-summarise control loop over engineering document retrieval.

Evaluation across 200 annotated queries spanning two industrial domains---hydraulic product catalogues and revision-controlled aerospace documentation---showed statistically significant improvements: answer correctness increased from 45\% to 85\% (Case~I) and from 23\% to 81\% (Case~II), while hallucination decreased from 55\% to 5\% and from 47\% to 11\% respectively, even with a compact 3B-parameter language model. The framework preserved engineering-critical fidelity in unit correctness, applicability scope, and provenance granularity, while transforming potential hallucinations into explicit, auditable failures.

Future work will focus on (i)~expanding validation toward cross-source consistency checking, including conflict detection and supersession-aware reconciliation across revisions; (ii)~improving multimodal grounding for tables and figures through tighter cell- and region-level anchoring; (iii)~incorporating cross-session learning to adapt strategy selection based on historical execution outcomes; and (iv)~developing evaluation suites that score provenance completeness, revision fidelity, and replay determinism alongside answer correctness.

The results indicate that treating orchestration as structured, persistent data provides a practical and extensible foundation for deploying reliable generative-AI systems in engineering knowledge management.
