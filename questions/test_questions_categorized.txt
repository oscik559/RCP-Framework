Question 33:
  Column 1: What is the difference between a 2SN and 2SC hose?
  Column 2: The difference between a 2SN and a 2SC hose lies primarily in construction and flexibility: 2SN: Has a standard outer diameter and is built according to EN 853 2SN. It is robust and is often used where there is space for a slightly thicker hose. 2SC: Has a more compact construction and smaller outer diameter than 2SN, but the same pressure class. 2SC is more flexible and is particularly suitable where it is cramped or where tighter bend radii are required. Both hose types are double steel wire reinforced hydraulic hoses for medium to high pressures, but 2SC offers advantages when installing in tight spaces.

Question 47:
  Column 1: The flow is 150 liters per minute, what hose dimension should I choose for pressure?
  Column 2: To dimension a pressure hose to a flow of 150 liters per minute, you need to take into account both flow rate and pressure drop. A common recommendation for hydraulics is to keep the flow rate below about 4–5 m/s for pressure hoses to minimize pressure drop and wear. Example of dimensioning: At 150 l/min and a desired flow rate of max. 5 m/s: 1" (25 mm internal diameter) hose gives about 5.1 m/s (suitable for most applications) 1 1/4" (32 mm internal diameter) hose gives about 3.7 m/s (even lower pressure drop and longer service life) Recommendation: Choose at least 1" (25 mm internal diameter) for 150 l/min at normal pressure. If you want to minimize pressure drop and have extra margin, choose 1 1/4" (32 mm internal diameter). Always also check the hose's pressure class against your system pressure.

Question 49:
  Column 1: The flow is 100 liters per minute and I can have a maximum pressure drop of 200 millibars, what hose dimension?
  Column 2: To keep the pressure drop below 200 millibar (0.2 bar) at 100 liters per minute, a relatively large hose size is required, especially if it is a longer hose and normal hydraulic oil viscosity. Tables or calculation programs are often used for this type of sizing, but a rule of thumb is: 1" (25 mm internal diameter) hose gives approximately 3.4–4 m/s at 100 l/min. The pressure drop per meter is often around 0.1–0.2 bar, depending on the hose type and length. 1 1/4" (32 mm internal diameter) hose gives approximately 2 m/s at 100 l/min and the pressure drop is significantly lower, often below 0.05 bar per meter. Recommendation: To comfortably handle a maximum pressure drop of 0.2 bar at 100 l/min, choose at least 1 1/4" (32 mm internal diameter) hose, especially if the hose is longer than a few meters or if you want extra security against pressure drop. Note: The exact pressure drop is affected by the length of the hose, bends, couplings and the viscosity of the oil. For longer hoses or higher viscosity, an even larger dimension may be required.

Question 70:
  Column 1: What is ISO bar?
  Column 2: "ISO bar" is not an official pressure unit or standard, but is often an informal expression used in the hose industry and hydraulics to indicate that a pressure value is specified in bar according to some ISO standard. In practice, this means: Bar is a pressure unit where 1 bar = 100 kPa = 0.1 MPa = approx. 14.5 psi. ISO refers to the fact that the pressure specification or product complies with a relevant ISO standard (e.g. ISO 4413 for hydraulics, ISO 1307 for hoses). If a hose or component is marked with "ISO bar", it usually means that it is tested and classified according to an ISO standard and that the working pressure is specified in bar. It is therefore not a separate pressure unit, but a combination of the pressure being specified in bar and the product being ISO certified or tested according to an ISO standard.

Question 77:
  Column 1: How do I install a shell sleeve?
  Column 2: The installation of a crimp sleeve (press sleeve for hydraulic hose that requires stripping) is done according to the following steps: Cut the hose perpendicularly with a suitable tool. Strip the hose according to the manufacturer's instructions. This means that you remove the outer rubber layer (and sometimes also the inner layer) to a certain length so that the reinforcement is exposed. Use a stripping tool or stripping device for the correct length and depth. Slide the crimp sleeve over the hose until it is seated against the end. Install the nipple/coupling in the hose. Make sure that it goes in to the correct depth (sometimes there is a stop). Press the coupling and crimp sleeve together with a hydraulic press according to the manufacturer's pressing dimensions. Check that the pressing is correct (no deformation, correct diameter, no visible damage). Important: Always follow the hose and coupling manufacturer's instructions for stripping dimensions and pressing dimensions. Use the correct tool to avoid damage to the reinforcement. Incorrect installation can lead to leakage or hose breakage.
